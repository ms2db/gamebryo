Simple ABV Hierarchy in 3ds Max

Now we'll look at how to structure the hierarchy in 3ds Max in more detail.

Sample Hierarchy for Simple ABV Setup

Above is an example of what our schematic view of a hierarchy might look like for creating an ABV out of 3ds Max. Of course, in the above case, we have a sphere mesh, so this is about as perfect case for using ABVs as you can get!

In order for the proxy ABVs to affect the proper nodes, you need to attach the proxies as children of the nodes for whom you want them to define ABVs. In this way, not only does the ABV generator know who to attach the ABV to, but the proxies will also inherit any transformations you may give to the parents, so the ABV proxies will remain in the proper position in relation to the parents while the model is being manipulated.

When working with a character, for instance, you may want to define the ABVs while the character is in figure mode so that the boxes are axis-aligned for the most part while you are defining them. The 3ds max schematic view of a Sphere ABV proxy type is shown above, as an example. The first child of the root node is the actual geometry for that node, and the second child is the ABV description, each of which has a single Box child and a basic name for ABV generation.

The location and orientation of the axes of the proxy are unimportant. One may want to align them with those of the object the box is attached to for clarity's sake, if desired, but the box will maintain its orientation to the parent no matter what these values are.
