Anisotropic Filtering.max

This 3ds Max sample illustrates the benefits of anisotropic filtering (AF). This type of filtering is beneficial because it mitigates the anisotropic effect that occurs when a sloped surface is viewed nearly on-edge. Without this filtering, the distance in texture space between the samples for two adjacent pixels becomes increasingly anisotropic (dependent on direction). Anisotropic filtering performs multiple regular samples (often called taps), taking the slope of the surface into account.

The sample shows how to add an anisotropic filtering texture property. Each of the planes has a custom texturing property with different settings:

  • Trilinear (trilerp) filtering
  • Anisotropic filtering (maximum of 2 taps*)
  • Anisotropic filtering (maximum of 16 taps*)

* Depending on the platform and graphics driver settings, the actual number of taps used may end up being lower than your setting. Check each renderer's release notes for the maximum number of taps supported in hardware (if the count is greater than supported, the maximum number supported will be used). Programmers may also enforce a lower maximum limit if desired.

On the plane with trilinear filtering (left), you can see heavy blurring in the distance. This blurring is lessened in the 2x AF plane (middle), and is lessened further in the 16x AF plane (right). Note how clear the yellow line is in the distance in the 16x AF plane.

By default, this sample installs to the following location:

%EMERGENT_PATH%\Samples\Models\Max Artist Examples\AnisotropicFiltering.max

Anisotropic Filtering sample

Note

Anisotropic filtering can be extremely expensive (a good rule of thumb is that N tap AF is nearly N times as expensive as trilinear filtering), and should only be used on surfaces where it makes a significant visual impact. It is often best to apply AF in a reactive manner, applying it only to surfaces that you notice getting very blurred in a typical game scenario.
