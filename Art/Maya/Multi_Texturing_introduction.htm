Multi-Texturing Introduction

At a high level, multi-texturing is the use of more than one texture simultaneously on the same set of polygons. By combining the multiple textures in different ways, subtle or striking effects can be created. These effects will be described below and include (but are not limited to) pseudo-lighting effects, shadows, and overlaid details.

There are two basic forms of multi-textures in Gamebryo—static multi-textures and projected textures. An easy way to understand the difference between the two is by a simple physical analogy. Static multi-textures represent surface properties of the objects to which they are attached. Projected textures represent spatial phenomena such as the reflected environment or a shaft of light coming from a window in the scene.

The basic technical distinction between the two classes is just as simple to understand. Static multi-textures are setup using the NiMultiShader material and are mapped using texture coordinates defined in those objects. Projected Textures are applied to a set of objects via a light-like inclusion list mechanism and are mapped to the geometry via dynamically-generated texture coordinates that are computed inside the low-level renderer.

This texture type. . . Creates this effect. . .
Base texture The basic appearance of the surface of a diffusely reflective object
Dark Map A representation of the diffused lighting on an object
Decal Map(s) Decals laid upon the surface of an object
Detail Map A high-frequency texture that alleviates the blurriness of objects seen close-up
Glow Map A representation of the emissive quality of the object surface – used to create the impression of glowing lights on the surface of the object
Gloss Map Modulates the effects of an environment map
Bump Map Appearance of bumps
