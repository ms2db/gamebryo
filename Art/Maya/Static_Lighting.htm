Static Lighting

There are many ways to optimize your lights so it looks like you have many lights in your scene when you only have one or two dynamic lights.  This technique is called Static lighting.  You "burn" lighting into your geometry on a vertex or pixel level ahead of time, giving it the appearance of being lit.  This technique is how most engines get such nice lighting while running at a decent frame rate. Static lighting can be done on a vertex level by painting the vertices or on a pixel level when you need very detailed shadows by assigning dark or light maps to your materials.

Use vertex colors.  By setting vertex colors, you can light each individual object uniquely without having to consider the expense of many dynamic lights.  The drawback of this feature is that your mesh may need more tessellation to prevent artifacts of the vertex lighting from showing.

Use dark maps.  In this case, you can use a small texture with tiling on the whole surface of an object, and use a separate texture (your dark map) once across the same surface.  In general, the need to have a highly detailed dark map is not very common, so this technique can be a great solution.

When lights are not moving in a scene, try to avoid dynamic lighting. Use vertex colors or dark maps. 

Burn the complex lighting ahead of time by creating your lights normally. Use a Max or Maya tool that is specialized to burn your lighting into the geometry like "Assign vertex colors", "Vertex paint" or "Advanced lighting \ Radiosity Solution". Set the material to show the finalized dark maps or vertex colors. For completely static geometry the mode in the Gamebryo Shader should be set to "Replace". You can then delete your original lighting, add a few additional dynamic lights to take care of interactive elements and end up with a fast but beautiful scene.
