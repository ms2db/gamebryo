The Basics of Cell-Portal Systems

The cell-portal visibility method is a system that allows interior scenes to be drawn very efficiently by representing not only the basic geometry of the interior scene, but also the high-level structure, in terms of visibility. In an interior scene, the objects we render are the rooms and their contents. While we could simply turn on z-buffering and render every room every frame, this would be wasteful in most cases. For example, if the camera was located in a room and was looking at a wall with no doorways, it is worthless to render all of the other rooms in the scene—architecturally, there is no way to see these rooms.

Cell-portal visibility systems are an example of the fact that every bit of high-level knowledge that can be encoded into a scene can allow for optimizations. Viewed as unstructured geometry, a scene must be rendered in its entirety every frame. Viewed as a hierarchical scene graph with culling spheres, the parts of the scene outside of the limits of the screen can be culled quickly. Add in information about the grouping of scene geometry into cells, along with visibility connections between these cells, and entire cells within the field of view, but not visible through doorways can be culled.

The basic building blocks of a cell-portal (or room-portal) system are the cells and the portals. A cell is the geometry that represents a room and everything in it, along with references to the doorways that lead out of that room. Portals represent these doorways, specifically the shape and location of the doorways (which tell us how much we can see out of the doorway from our current position) and a reference to the room that is seen through the doorway.
