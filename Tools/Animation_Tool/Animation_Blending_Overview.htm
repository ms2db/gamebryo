Animation Blending Overview

In modern games and simulations, it is often desirable to have two or more animations running simultaneously on the same character. For instance, when transitioning from a walk animation to a run animation, there will be a period in which the actor's movement will be somewhere between the walk and the run. When two or more animations are simultaneously affecting a character, animation blending must occur. The amount that each animation contributes to the blend is called its weight. In the case of the walk/run transition, the walk's weight is originally 1 and the run's weight is 0. As time passes, the animation system will ramp the walk's weight down to 0 and the run's weight will ramp up to 1. Halfway through the blending operation, walk will be at 0.5 and run at 0.5. This example is a very simple kind of blend known as a transition. A transition is a blend from one animation to another with the intent of going from a source animation to a destination animation.

Another animation blending example is mixing multiple look animations. The actor could be looking directly above, directly below, to the left, and to the right. It is possible to activate all four animation sequences simultaneously and allow their weights to determine in which direction the actor will look. If a looking up and right animation is required, the weights would be as follows: 0.5 up, 0.0 down, 0.5 right, 0.0 left. If the target moves to down and left, the weights can be altered over time to be 0.0 up, 0.5 down, 0.0 right, and 0.5 left. The actor will move smoothly from one combination of animations to the other combination as the weights are altered. Traditional IK approaches to this problem would look unrealistic as simply moving the end effector of the head IK chain does not necessarily move the upper body. When an actor looks up or down in reality, they must alter their stance and torso. Better results can be accomplished using blending since the artist maintains full control over the upper body.

 

The "blend space" of Look Sequences

A third example of animation blending is walking and shooting. An artist could create a shoot animation and a walk cycle. These two can be blended together programmatically. This would have the effect of watching the actor walk, fire a few shots while still walking, then go back into swinging his arms for the walk cycle. The same shooting animation could be blended on top of a run cycle. This form of animation blending is useful in reducing the number of permutations of animations an artist is required to generate.

A fourth example of animation blending is combining multiple "adverb" animations to augment the basic "verb" animation. Think of a plain walk cycle as an action, or verb. In print, you can augment the description of the verb, "walk" with adverbs. For example, "he walked happily" or "he walked sadly". The adverbs of "happily" and "sadly" can be expressed as different versions of the walk animation cycle. Mixing the two examples produces a spectrum of animations in the emotional range between "happy" and "sad". Further variations can be provided in the form of "injured" to "healthy" or "intelligent" to "stupid".
